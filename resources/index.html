// How EcoViewer is organised

Biodiversity &
environmental layers

EcoViewer is organised around two complementary types of data. The first is ecosystem indicator species occurrence data from GBIF, matched to ecosystem types and biomes. The second is a set of environmental layers describing the conditions of a location — its climate, vegetation, water, and fire history.

Select a location on the map and switch between panels to explore both dimensions together. All layers are derived from peer-reviewed, publicly available datasets processed through Google Earth Engine. Full citations are listed in the sections below.

01

Ecosystem indicator species occurrence records from the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF), matched to ecosystem types — or to biomes where a specific ecosystem type cannot be determined — using peer-reviewed ecological literature. The presence of these species can signal the likely occurrence of a particular ecosystem type at a location.

// GBIF Ecosystem Indicator Species

Connecting biodiversity
to ecosystem types

EcoViewer integrates species occurrence records from the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) — the largest open repository of biodiversity data in the world. Not all species in GBIF can be linked to a specific ecosystem type. EcoViewer focuses on ecosystem indicator species — species whose physiological characteristics allow them to be successful in specific Ecosystem Functional Groups and help shape those ecosystems.

Each occurrence record is matched to an Ecosystem Functional Group, or to a broader biome where a specific group cannot be reliably determined. The methodology is built on a growing body of peer-reviewed literature. A selection of key references is listed below.

// What is an Ecosystem Functional Group?

The IUCN Global Ecosystem Typology (GET) classifies all of Earth's ecosystems into a six-level hierarchy. An Ecosystem Functional Group is the third level — a group of related ecosystems within a biome that share common ecological drivers, promoting similar biotic traits. For example, mangrove forests are an Ecosystem Functional Group: distinct from tropical rainforests or seagrass beds, yet sharing the intertidal realm with other coastal wetland types.

// What is an ecosystem indicator species?

We define ecosystem indicator species as species whose physiological characteristics allow them to thrive in specific Ecosystem Functional Groups and help shape those ecosystems. For example, mangrove tree species (Rhizophora spp.) have adaptations that allow them to survive at the interface of marine, freshwater, and terrestrial realms — and in doing so, they build and maintain intertidal forests.

Important caveat: the presence of an ecosystem indicator species does not mean that species is only found in that Ecosystem Functional Group, nor that the ecosystem is definitively present at that location. Rather, it indicates a higher likelihood of finding that ecosystem type there. Occurrence records should be interpreted as signals, not definitive maps.

// Environmental Layers

Climate, vegetation,
water & fire data

Alongside GBIF occurrence data, EcoViewer provides a set of global environmental layers describing the conditions at any location on Earth — climate, vegetation structure, surface water and coastal extent, and fire disturbance history.

These layers are derived from established, peer-reviewed global datasets, harmonised and processed through Google Earth Engine. All datasets are publicly available and freely accessible. The table below lists each dataset, its provider, licence, and relevant references.
